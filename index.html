A small treatise on agentic craft

The Two New Arts of AI

One art gives attention a shape. The other turns knowledge into action.

As AI systems become collaborators, a new kind of work is appearing: not only making content, but designing the conditions under which people and agents can think, choose, and act.

Proemium Opening

AI is changing the shape of skilled work. The obvious story is speed: faster writing, faster design, faster research, faster code. But the deeper story is craft. When machines can generate and execute, human work moves upstream toward judgment, framing, taste, and the design of useful constraints.

Two crafts are emerging: the art of shaping attention, and the art of distilling knowledge into skills an AI agent can use.

An agent is not just a search box or a blank page. It can follow instructions, call tools, remember context, and take steps toward a goal. That changes what knowledge must become. A course, a workshop, or a professional habit is no longer finished when it has been taught. It may also need to be refined into a repeatable capacity.

Ars I. Sculptura Attentionis

The Attention Sculptor

An attention sculptor is not someone who simply grabs attention. That is the crude art of interruption. The finer art is to remove what does not belong, arrange what remains, and make a field of choices easier to see.

The material is not stone. It is sequence, naming, contrast, rhythm, defaults, silence, and friction. A well-shaped experience does not make people stare longer. It helps them notice the right thing, at the right scale, for the decision in front of them.

Lens

The Lens

Focuses scattered signals into one question a reader, user, or team can actually hold.

Chisel

The Chisel

Cuts away useful-looking noise so the structure of the problem can appear.

Frame

The Frame

Sets the scale of comparison: time, cost, risk, evidence, responsibility, or consequence.

Tempo

The Tempo

Decides when to move quickly and when to slow the mind before judgment hardens.

Ars II. Oenologia Cognitionis

The Knowledge Distiller

The second craft begins where ordinary training ends. A curriculum, a playbook, a lecture, a consulting method, or a master practitioner's instinct may contain real knowledge, but it is not yet an agent skill. It is still raw material.

To distill knowledge is to extract the active ingredients: when to use a method, what to ask first, which exceptions matter, how to judge quality, and where the method should stop. The result is not a summary. It is a small vessel of action.

Materia Source Knowledge

Courses, cases, notes, demonstrations, expert habits, and hard-earned shortcuts.

Fermentum Judgment

Triggers, exceptions, examples, counterexamples, quality bars, and stopping rules.

Elixir Agent Skill

A callable, testable, revisable unit of capability that can be combined with other tools.

Objectiones

Cautions: Where the Metaphor Breaks

These images are useful because they are beautiful, but they are also dangerous. Attention sculpture can become manipulation. Knowledge distillation can become overcompression. A living practice can be reduced to a recipe that looks precise because its doubts have been stripped away.

The remedy is not to abandon the crafts. It is to give them ethics and maintenance. People need room to exit, question, and reframe. Agent skills need provenance, boundaries, tests, and a way to expire.

Over-Sculpting

When an interface decides too much, clarity becomes domestication. Good attention design increases agency.

Dead Recipes

When training is compressed too hard, hesitation, failure cases, and tacit judgment are lost first.

Single Palate

A distiller has taste, and taste has bias. A skill should not hide one expert's preferences as universal truth.

Amplificatio

The Guild Around the Agent

The two arts imply a wider guild. Attention sculptors shape the entrance to thought. Knowledge distillers turn experience into executable capacity. But they need companions: assayers who test whether a skill works, and cellar keepers who preserve context over time.

Without testing, a skill is only a convincing spell. Without maintenance, even a good skill can grow stale. The future of agentic work will depend on craft, but also on evaluation, versioning, provenance, and care.

Craft Material Good Output Failure Mode
Attention Sculpture Entrances, order, contrast, pauses A path for judgment Addiction or control
Knowledge Distillation Courses, cases, expert habits A callable agent skill A brittle recipe
Assay Tasks, edge cases, real feedback A trustworthy quality boundary Teaching to the test
Cellaring Versions, sources, use contexts A living knowledge cellar Expired skills in circulation

Examen Lectoris

A Reader's Test

The test of this framework is not whether the alchemical style is charming. The test is whether a reader leaves with distinctions that can be used the next day.

  1. Insight: AI work is not only about producing more output. It is also about shaping attention and packaging judgment.
  2. Readability: Each craft has a plain job: one helps people see; the other helps agents act.
  3. Critique: The framework names its own shadows: manipulation, overcompression, stale knowledge, and hidden bias.

The most useful question is no longer only “What can AI replace?” It is also “What forms of human craft become more important when intelligence can be called as a tool?”
