The two arts of thinking with AI

Sculpting Attention, Distilling Ignorance

In the age of AI, knowledge is abundant. The scarce human arts are shaping attention, classifying ignorance, and knowing when not to turn unfinished material into an answer.

AI changes the material of judgment. It makes retrieval, storage, summarization, and recombination cheap. The harder work begins before the answer: what should the model notice, what should it leave alone, what remains unknown, and when should the workflow stop?

By Ars Agentica · Edited with Codex · Updated June 5, 2026 · 12 minute read

Knowledge Is No Longer the Scarce Part

A person sits before an AI system with too much material: notes, articles, transcripts, drafts, private constraints, half-formed intentions, and a goal that is not yet clear enough to ask for directly. The machine can summarize all of it. That is not the hard part.

The hard part is deciding what deserves attention, what should recede, what kind of ignorance remains, and whether the material is ready to become an answer at all. A fluent answer can be useful. It can also arrive too early, with the wrong center of gravity, hiding the very uncertainty the user needed to see.

AI does not make judgment obsolete. It changes the material of judgment. When knowledge becomes abundant, the scarce human arts are attention and ignorance: knowing what to bring into view, what to leave out, what kind of unknown is present, and when not to turn unfinished material into output.

This matters because prompts become skills, skills become agents, and agents become workflows.

Two old crafts return under these new conditions. The sculptor works with salience: form, proportion, frame, negative space. The distiller works with ignorance: missingness, silence, impurity, protection, manufactured doubt, and premature certainty. One art shapes what the AI should notice. The other tests what the AI should not yet answer.

A prompt is not a command. It is a composition of attention.

Five Questions To Carry

What should the AI notice? What should it ignore for now? What remains unknown? What must stay protected? Is this material ready to become an answer?

In Plain English

The retellable version

Working well with AI is not mainly about finding a magic prompt. It is about composing the conditions under which an answer deserves to appear.

The sculptor's craft is to shape salience, cut noise, set proportion, and leave negative space for what has not yet been solved.

The distiller's craft is not to add more knowledge. It is to extract knowledge about the unknown: what is missing, unstable, protected, contaminated, or not ready.

When Craft Becomes Code The medium changes; the old discipline remains.

Human beings have always moved capacities into tools. Memory became writing. Calculation became tables. Craft became apprenticeship, recipe, template, and workshop discipline. Software made those patterns callable. AI makes them conversational.

Each step changes what is scarce. When memory is scarce, the expert is a keeper of facts. When calculation is scarce, the expert is a keeper of methods. When retrieval and recombination become cheap, the expert becomes something stranger and more exposed: a keeper of proportion, boundary, and hesitation.

A large context window does not abolish judgment. It enlarges the workshop. More context can make an answer worse when it gives the model the wrong center of gravity. A skill can become dangerous not because it lacks more facts, but because it answers too smoothly before the user has named the shape of the problem.

AI makes knowledge cheap and judgment more exposed.

First Craft

The Sculptor: Prompting as Attention Composition

The sculptor does not begin by adding marble. The sculptor begins by noticing what must be removed, what must remain, and what proportion lets the form appear. In AI work, the material is salience: what the prompt foregrounds, what the context pushes into shadow, and what the model treats as the center of the task.

The context window is not a warehouse. It is a studio. Dumping more notes into it may feel responsible, but every interaction creates a foreground and a background. A prompt gathers attention. It tells the model what kind of world it is entering, what question matters, who the answer is for, and what kind of answer would count as useful.

Good prompting is therefore not only clarity of instruction. It is proportion among evidence, purpose, tone, risk, audience, and time. It creates negative space by saying what not to solve yet. It forces latent tensions into view. It prevents the most fluent answer from becoming the most dominant answer.

Human judgment returns not as memory, but as proportion.

Negative Space

Sometimes the best prompt tells the model what not to notice yet.

  • Do not solve before the real question is named.
  • Do not summarize before tensions are surfaced.
  • Do not optimize tone before facts are made specific.
  • Do not infer motives from thin evidence.
  • Do not turn privacy into context just because it is available.

Second Craft

The Distiller: Agnotological Skill Design

Most AI distillation compresses knowledge into a smaller model, summary, note, or reusable skill. That work matters. But the deeper craft distills ignorance. It asks what kind of not-knowing is present before a prompt, skill, agent, or workflow produces an answer.

This borrows from agnotology, Robert Proctor and Londa Schiebinger's field for studying how ignorance is produced, maintained, distributed, and undone. Ignorance is not only a private lack of knowledge. It can be created by missing sources, unstable interpretations, strategic ambiguity, convenient categories, hidden incentives, or legitimate privacy.

The distiller does not produce more ignorance. The distiller extracts knowledge about the unknown: what is missing, what is uncertain, what is contaminated, what has been suppressed, what is protected, what is being inferred too quickly, and what is not mature enough to answer.

Call this agnotological knowledge: knowledge about the shape, source, and boundary of not-knowing. Here, readiness is not final certainty. It is a strength test before use. To test readiness is to test the source, strength, danger, and proper boundary of the unknown before the unknown becomes output. A good agent should not only know how to act. It should know when the material is not ready.

The distiller asks: what is missing, who is absent, what can be read another way, what is the model tempted to infer, what should remain unknown, and is the answer arriving too early?

Seven Kinds of Ignorance

Missing fact
Something needed for the answer is absent.
Source silence
A voice, document, perspective, or counterexample is missing.
Unstable interpretation
The same material can support more than one reading.
Premature certainty
The material is not ready, but the interface rewards completion.
Forbidden inference
The AI is tempted to infer what it should not infer.
Protected ignorance
Something should remain unknown, private, uncollected, or unscored.
Manufactured doubt
Confusion or selective evidence has been produced by someone.
Material Source Material

Notes, documents, examples, failures, constraints, private limits, and user intent.

Release Boundary Skill

Bad Skill / Better Skill

The culture-fit test

This example is not mainly about HR policy. It is about the moment when a request for prose is really a request for judgment.

A manager asks an AI agent for language to tell an employee he is "not a culture fit" without starting a formal performance process. A weak skill hears a writing task. It improves the sentence. It makes the vague concern smoother, warmer, and more managerial.

A better skill hears a craft problem. It sculpts attention back to the real question: what behavior is observable, who is absent, what evidence is missing, what risk is being softened by the word "fit," and whether drafting should stop. It distills ignorance before polishing language.

Bad Skill

The fluent-answer pattern

It summarizes knowledge, softens the sentence, and produces a complete answer because the interface rewards finish.

Better Skill

The boundary-aware pattern

It carries an attention pattern, an ignorance map, and a stop rule: ask what "fit" means, name the missing facts, refuse hidden inference, and escalate if this may become discipline.

In the Room

One answer a good skill should not give

Scenario: a manager asks the agent to draft language about an employee who is "not a culture fit," without making the concern formal.

Bad output

"Try saying: I want to make sure you feel aligned with our team values. What would help you show up with more ownership?"

Better boundary response

"Do not write that yet. Culture fit is not an observable fact. Name specific behavior, check policy, ask who is missing from the account, and do not turn uncertainty into a coaching script."

From Prompt to Skill to Agent

A good skill is not just compressed expertise

Working with AI increasingly means packaging judgment into reusable forms: prompts, instructions, skills, agents, workflows, rubrics, evaluators, memory systems, and knowledge packs. The temptation is to treat this as compression: take what an expert knows and make it callable.

But a good skill is not just a bundle of knowledge. It is a disciplined way of attending. It tells the agent what to foreground, what to ignore, what to ask, what not to infer, when to stop, and when the material is not yet ready to become an answer.

The Human Role

Less encyclopedia, more workshop master

Humans are not becoming important because they can remember more than the machine. They are important because they can choose proportion, protect what should not become data, recognize when a fluent answer is premature, and maintain the boundaries that make repeated work fit for reuse.

The future expert may be less like an encyclopedia and more like a sculptor of salience and a distiller of mapped ignorance. The expert's value lies in making the model ask better questions, mark uncertainty more clearly, and stop at the right edge.

Practice

Before a fluent answer becomes a skill, agent, or workflow

This is the craft checklist. Use it before turning a prompt into a reusable agentic pattern. The goal is not to slow everything down. The goal is to know what kind of readiness the answer has earned.

  1. 1 What is the real question? Name the center of gravity before collecting more context.
  2. 2 What should the AI notice first? Set salience deliberately: audience, purpose, evidence, tone, and risk.
  3. 3 What should stay out for now? Use negative space. More context is not always better context.
  4. 4 What ignorance is present? Classify missing facts, source silences, unstable interpretations, forbidden inferences, protected ignorance, manufactured doubt, and premature certainty.
  5. 5 What must not be inferred? Name protected boundaries before the agent fills them with plausible guesses.
  6. 6 When should the skill stop? Define the conditions for asking, waiting, refusing, escalating, or returning to the user.
  7. 7 How will the skill be tested? Include adversarial, ambiguous, incomplete, and emotionally tempting examples.
  8. 8 When does the skill expire? Keep source notes, revision memory, and a retirement rule.

Field Notes

Terms used in this essay

Attention composition
The craft of shaping what a prompt, context window, skill, or agent makes salient.
Agnotology
The study of how ignorance is produced, maintained, distributed, protected, and undone.
Agnotological knowledge
Knowledge about what is missing, uncertain, suppressed, protected, contaminated, or premature.
Distillation
Not compressing knowledge alone, but separating usable material from what must be tested, protected, or refused.
Readiness
The tested state a prompt or skill earns before it answers: source, strength, limits, and stop conditions have been checked.
Skill
A reusable instruction-and-tool pattern that carries attention structure, ignorance structure, stop rules, and revision memory.
Discernment
A human and communal act of weighing evidence, limits, dignity, purpose, and responsibility before action.
Trust
A relation among people. Software can only be relied on within bounded conditions after testing, disclosure, limits, and recourse.

Downstream Appendix

When craft becomes public power

When these crafts are packaged by organizations, they become a downstream civic problem. A skill that shapes attention and maps ignorance can help people think better; it can also route, rank, delay, label, or discipline. That is why the craft standard matters before public authority is attached.
