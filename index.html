A public essay on agentic craft

The Two New Arts of AI

AI makes expertise portable. That is the promise, and the danger.

The scarce skill is no longer only producing more information. It is deciding what deserves attention, and what can safely become action.

The Scene

A leadership coach has a ten-week course, a stack of case studies, and a way of pausing when a client says something evasive. A company asks whether that expertise can become an AI agent. The slides are easy to upload. The judgment is harder.

The agent needs to know when to probe, when to summarize, when to refuse a shortcut, and when a confident answer would be worse than a careful question. The work is not merely digitizing instruction. It is preserving judgment while making it portable.

Portability is not the same as wisdom.

In Plain English

The argument

AI creates new crafts, not only job replacement. Human work moves toward framing, constraint, taste, testing, and maintenance.

Attention sculpture shapes how people notice and decide. Knowledge distillation turns expertise into agent skills.

But when judgment becomes portable, context can be stripped away. That is why the craft needs ethics, evaluation, and versioning.

Ars I. Sculptura Attentionis

The Attention Sculptor

The first craft is often mistaken for persuasion. It is not. A good attention sculptor is not trying to make people stare longer. The work is to arrange a field of facts so that a person can see the decision inside it.

In a product dashboard, this may mean showing the anomaly before the archive. In a classroom, it may mean delaying the answer until the student has formed a better question. In public discourse, it may mean giving tradeoffs a shape before outrage fills the room.

Attention design is legitimate only when it increases the reader's agency.

The Chisel

Ars II. Oenologia Cognitionis

The Knowledge Distiller

The second craft begins where training usually stops. A curriculum, a playbook, a lecture, or a master practitioner's instinct may contain real knowledge, but it is not yet an agent skill. It is still raw material.

To distill knowledge is to extract the active ingredients: when to use a method, what to ask first, which exceptions matter, how to judge quality, and where the method should stop. The result is not a summary. It is a small vessel of action.

Materia Source Knowledge

Courses, cases, notes, demonstrations, expert habits, and hard-earned shortcuts.

Fermentum Judgment

Triggers, exceptions, examples, counterexamples, quality bars, and stopping rules.

Elixir Agent Skill

A callable, testable, revisable unit of capability that can be combined with other tools.

Three Small Cases

Where the crafts appear

The idea becomes more useful when it leaves the diagram. These are not science-fiction jobs. They are ordinary knowledge-work situations with a new layer of responsibility.

Education

A course becomes an agent skill

A negotiation workshop becomes useful to an agent only after someone names the intake questions, concession rules, danger signs, sample messages, and failure cases. The stakes are not speed alone; the stakes are whether students inherit the teacher's judgment or merely the teacher's slides.

Product Design

A dashboard becomes attention sculpture

A growth dashboard can drown a team in metrics. A sculpted one stages attention: first the anomaly, then the driver, then the question worth asking. It does not remove complexity. It gives complexity an order in which a decision can be made.

Operations

A playbook becomes an evaluated workflow

A customer-support guide becomes an agent only when escalation criteria, audit logs, risky examples, and refusal rules are made explicit. The hidden craft is not answering the easy tickets. It is knowing which tickets should not be automated.

Instrumentum

Lens Alembic Crucible Cellar Judgment

I. Lens

Shape attention before action.

What should appear first? What can wait? What must remain visible as a warning?

II. Alembic

Distill expertise into conditions.

Good skills are made from triggers, exceptions, examples, boundaries, and quality bars.

III. Crucible

Test outside the easy cases.

A skill that works only on friendly examples is not a skill. It is a demonstration.

IV. Cellar

Keep sources, versions, and expiration dates.

Portable expertise needs memory: where it came from, when it changed, and when it should retire.

What Is at Stake

The public argument

If expertise becomes portable, more people can reach useful knowledge. That is the promise. But if portability strips away context, institutions may distribute confidence without responsibility. That is the danger.

Education

Do students get a tutor with judgment, or a machine that repeats the official answer?

Management

Do teams inherit better decision rituals, or a new layer of automated certainty?

Public Discourse

Do interfaces slow people down enough to understand tradeoffs, or merely route attention toward reaction?

Professional Work

Do agents extend expertise, or flatten the difference between a method and a person who knows when to abandon it?

Objectiones

Some knowledge should resist automation

The argument has a shadow. Not every form of knowledge should become an agent skill. Some expertise depends on trust, presence, liability, or moral hesitation. Some work should be assisted by AI without being delegated to it.

The remedy is not nostalgia. It is a higher standard. People need room to exit, question, and reframe. Agent skills need provenance, boundaries, tests, and a way to expire.

Manipulation

When an interface decides too much, clarity becomes domestication. Good attention design increases agency.

Overcompression

When training is compressed too hard, hesitation, failure cases, and tacit judgment are lost first.

False Universality

A distiller has taste, and taste has bias. A skill should not hide one expert's preferences as universal truth.

Glossarium

Small glossary

Agent Skill

A reusable instruction-and-tool pattern that lets an AI agent perform a bounded task.

Attention Sculpture

The design of what appears first, what waits, and how a person moves from noticing to deciding.

Knowledge Distillation

The conversion of expertise into triggers, procedures, examples, tests, and boundaries.

Assay / Evaluation

The test that reveals whether a skill works outside the easiest examples.

Cellaring / Versioning

The work of preserving sources, updating skills, and retiring methods that have gone stale.

Praxis

How to begin

The craft begins with one recurring task, not with a grand system. Choose something specific enough to test and important enough that better judgment would matter.

  1. 1 Choose a recurring task. Pick a workflow that happens often and has visible consequences when done badly.
  2. 2 Identify expert judgment. Ask what a skilled person notices, ignores, checks, and refuses to do.
  3. 3 Write triggers, exceptions, and quality bars. Turn intuition into conditions an agent can inspect and follow.
  4. 4 Test against real cases. Use failures, edge cases, and feedback to decide what the skill is allowed to claim.

Examen Lectoris

A reader's test

The test of this essay is not whether the alchemical style is charming. The test is whether a reader leaves with a sharper question for the next AI project.

  1. Insight: AI work is not only about producing more output. It is also about shaping attention and packaging judgment.
  2. Readability: Each craft has a plain job: one helps people see; the other helps agents act.
  3. Critique: The framework names its own shadows: manipulation, overcompression, stale knowledge, and hidden bias.

The useful question is no longer only “What can AI replace?” It is also “What forms of human craft become more important when intelligence can be called as a tool?”
