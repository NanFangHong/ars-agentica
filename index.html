The two new arts of AI, recast as a public question

What Should Expertise Be Allowed to Become?

As institutions turn judgment into software, the scarce skill is not automation.

It is knowing what must remain answerable to people: hesitation, provenance, refusal, appeal, and care.

By Ars Agentica · June 3, 2026 · 11 minute read

The Scene

On a Tuesday morning, a leadership coach opens the slide deck she has used for eight years. The company does not really want the slides. It wants the pause she makes before asking a second question. It wants the way she hears evasion in a perfectly reasonable answer.

The room is ordinary: a manager, an HR partner, a half-finished performance plan, and a Slack thread no one wants to put in the file. In one role-play, the manager says, “He is not a culture fit, but I do not want to make it formal yet.” A generic coaching agent can soften that sentence in a second. That is exactly the problem.

The coach does not reach for a better phrase. She asks what facts the manager has, whether the concern is observable, who might be harmed by vagueness, and whether the word “fit” is hiding bias inside taste. She asks when coaching becomes discipline, when discipline becomes liability, and what trust is owed to the employee who is not in the room. The pause is the lesson.

Most organizations will try to upload the manual and call it transformation. But an agent needs more than a manual. It needs to know when to probe, when to summarize, when to refuse a shortcut, and when a confident answer would be worse than a careful question. The two new arts of AI begin there: sculpting attention so judgment improves, and distilling knowledge so an agent knows where its authority ends.

Portability is not the same as wisdom.

In Plain English

The argument

AI creates new crafts, not only job replacement. Human work moves toward framing, constraint, taste, testing, and maintenance.

Attention sculpture shapes how people notice and decide. Knowledge distillation turns expertise into agent skills. A summary is not a skill.

When judgment becomes portable, power moves too. The question is who owns the hesitation that made the judgment wise.

Why Now The upload is no longer a metaphor.

Every organization is trying to turn expertise into software. A school district wants a tutoring agent. A hospital wants an intake assistant. A sales team wants the instincts of its best negotiator available on every call. The request sounds practical, even modest: take what our best people know and make it available everywhere.

The danger is not simply that experts disappear. The danger is that their judgment is copied without their hesitation. A method can travel faster than the context that made it wise. That is why the old question, “Will AI replace expertise?” is too blunt. The sharper question is what happens when expertise becomes portable before it becomes accountable.

When expertise becomes portable, who owns the hesitation that made it wise?

The Institution's Temptation

Scalable compliance dressed as wisdom

Organizations do not always want portable wisdom. Sometimes they want scalable compliance dressed as wisdom. The expert's hesitation is expensive, local, and hard to audit. The packaged skill is cheaper, consistent, and easy to deploy.

That temptation bends both crafts. Attention sculpture can become visibility control: show the metric that serves the institution, delay the fact that gives a person grounds to object. Knowledge distillation can become deskilling: turn a worker's judgment into a company asset, then deny the worker authority to challenge or revise it.

The public question is not only whether the agent performs well. It is whether the people affected by the agent can see the frame, contest the frame, and reach a human route without punishment.

First Craft

The Attention Sculptor

The first craft sounds like persuasion because both deal in attention. The difference is moral and practical. Persuasion tries to move a person toward a desired conclusion. Attention sculpture is legitimate only when it gives a person more power to judge.

In a product dashboard, this may mean showing the anomaly before the archive. In a classroom, it may mean delaying the answer until the student has formed a better question. In public discourse, it may mean giving tradeoffs a shape before outrage fills the room. If it hides alternatives or makes refusal costly, it is manipulation.

Attention design earns trust when the person can push back.

The Agency Test

Attention design is legitimate only when the person can push back.

  • Can the person see why something is shown first?
  • Can the person challenge the frame?
  • Can the person recover what was hidden or delayed?
  • Can the person choose a human route without penalty?
  • Is there a record of who changed the skill and why?

Second Craft

The Knowledge Distiller

The second craft begins where training usually stops. A curriculum, a playbook, a lecture, or a master practitioner's instinct may contain real knowledge, but it is not yet an agent skill. It is still raw material.

Return to the leadership coach. Her slides tell managers to ask open-ended questions. Her practice is more exacting: listen for the sentence that arrives too quickly, ask what would be at risk if the answer were true, and stop when the conversation crosses into therapy, discipline, or legal exposure. That is the difference between content and judgment.

To distill knowledge is to extract the active ingredients: when to use a method, what to ask first, which exceptions matter, how to judge quality, and where the method should stop. The result is not a summary. A summary describes what an expert knows. A skill tests whether an agent can act without pretending to know more than it does.

A serious skill also names its provenance, revision authority, appeal path, retirement date, and feedback from people affected by the agent. Otherwise, it is not distilled expertise. It is an institution speaking in an expert's voice.

Source Source Knowledge

Courses, cases, notes, demonstrations, expert habits, and hard-earned shortcuts.

Judgment Judgment

Triggers, exceptions, examples, counterexamples, quality bars, and stopping rules.

Skill Agent Skill

A callable, testable, revisable unit of capability that can be combined with other tools.

Bad Skill

The slide-deck agent

It summarizes the module, offers generic coaching prompts, and keeps answering even when the situation needs context it does not have.

Better Skill

The judgment-bearing agent

It asks what is at stake, names uncertainty, watches for power and liability, and escalates when the expert would stop rather than improvise.

In the Room

One answer an expert would not let pass

Scenario: a manager asks the agent for language to tell an employee he is “not a culture fit,” without starting a formal performance process.

Bad agent answer

“Try saying: I want to make sure you feel aligned with our team values. What would help you show up with more ownership?”

Better agent answer

“Pause before scripting this. Culture fit is vague and can hide bias. Name the observable facts, check policy, involve HR if this may become discipline, and do not frame a formal concern as casual coaching.”

Public Cases

Where people feel the agent

The idea becomes more useful when it leaves the diagram. These are not science-fiction jobs. They are public and workplace settings where people affected by the agent may not have chosen the agent.

School District

A tutoring agent meets a struggling student

Asked to do: help students practice algebra after school, explain mistakes, and recommend review exercises to teachers.

Expert hesitation: a teacher notices when a student is not confused about fractions but ashamed to admit a reading problem, a language gap, or a fear of being tracked.

What could go wrong: the agent turns struggle into a permanent label, hides uncertainty from parents, or routes students into lower expectations because the interface made that path easiest.

Boundary rule: the agent may practice and explain, but it cannot assign a placement, diagnose a disability, or quietly lower the standard without teacher and family review.

Hospital Intake

An assistant sorts urgency before a nurse sees the patient

Asked to do: collect symptoms, insurance details, recent medications, and warning signs before a patient reaches the desk.

Expert hesitation: an intake nurse knows that people minimize pain, translate symptoms poorly, or avoid saying what they fear because they worry about cost, immigration, or embarrassment.

What could go wrong: the agent over-trusts a calm answer, buries a dangerous symptom behind administrative fields, or makes a patient feel that escalation is a privilege.

Boundary rule: the agent may gather information and surface red flags, but it must escalate uncertainty, distress, chest pain, breathing trouble, neurological symptoms, or requests for a person.

Newsroom

An editorial assistant ranks what deserves attention

Asked to do: scan tips, public records, social posts, and wires, then suggest what an editor should read first.

Expert hesitation: an editor knows the difference between public interest and public appetite, and knows that vulnerable people can be harmed by a story that is technically true.

What could go wrong: the agent rewards outrage, repeats an official frame, or makes attention look neutral when it is really a ranking of institutional priorities.

Boundary rule: the agent may rank evidence and flag missing verification, but it cannot decide publication, name vulnerable people, or suppress a competing frame without an editor's recorded reason.

The Model

Focus Distill Crucible Maintain Agency

I. Focus

Shape attention before action.

What should appear first? What can wait? What must remain visible as a warning?

II. Distill

Distill expertise into conditions.

Good skills are made from triggers, exceptions, examples, boundaries, and quality bars.

III. Crucible

Test outside the easy cases.

A skill that works only on friendly examples is not a skill. It is a demonstration.

IV. Maintain

Keep sources, versions, and expiration dates.

Portable expertise needs memory: where it came from, when it changed, and when it should retire.

Public Accountability

Who gets a say?

If expertise becomes portable, more people can reach useful knowledge. That is the promise. But power also moves from the expert to the institution that packages the expert. The question is not only whether the agent is useful. It is who gets to decide when its confidence should stop.

A hospital intake desk, a school district, a newsroom, and a sales floor do not need the same agent wearing different costumes. They need different boundaries, different records of authority, and different ways for people to say no.

Four groups belong in the conversation: experts whose judgment is distilled, people who must work with the agent, people affected by the agent's decisions, and institutions that profit from portability.

Ownership

When a coach's judgment becomes a company skill, who is allowed to change it?

Liability

Who is responsible when an agent is confident in a case the expert would escalate?

Exit

Can workers, students, patients, or customers refuse the automated expert?

Maintenance

Who retires a method when law, evidence, culture, or context changes?

Cautions

What should not become a skill?

The argument has a shadow. Not every form of knowledge should become an agent skill. Some expertise depends on trust, presence, liability, or moral hesitation. Some work should be assisted by AI without being delegated to it.

The most valuable expertise may be the part that refuses to become portable. The remedy is not nostalgia. It is a higher standard: people need room to exit, question, and reframe. Agent skills need provenance, boundaries, tests, and a way to expire.

Counterargument

Is this just prompt engineering with better clothes?

Sometimes, yes. If the work stops at clever phrasing, the old name is enough. The phrase “knowledge distillation” earns its keep only when it changes the standard: a skill must carry examples, boundaries, evaluation, provenance, and a refusal rule. Otherwise it is only a prompt dressed as a profession.

Manipulation

When an interface decides too much, clarity becomes domestication. Good attention design increases agency.

Overcompression

When training is compressed too hard, hesitation, failure cases, and tacit judgment are lost first.

False Universality

A distiller has taste, and taste has bias. A skill should not hide one expert's preferences as universal truth.

Human Exposure

Therapy, hiring, discipline, medical triage, and legal pressure require more than portable technique.

Practice

Before You Automate the Expert

The craft begins with one recurring task, not with a grand system. Choose something specific enough to test and important enough that better judgment would matter. But the task should not move forward until the people building it can answer a few public questions.

  1. 1 Name the hesitation. What does the expert pause over, and what makes that pause legitimate?
  2. 2 List affected people. Include people affected by the agent, not only people operating it.
  3. 3 Publish provenance. Record sources, examples, version history, revision authority, and expiry dates.
  4. 4 Build refusal routes. Make escalation and human review ordinary, visible, and free of penalty.
  5. 5 Test the appeal. Use hard cases to check whether people can contest the frame and recover what was hidden.

Reader's Test

What the reader should be able to ask

The test of this essay is not whether the old-world style is charming. The test is whether, at the next meeting about AI, a reader can hear a better question forming before the usual one arrives.

First, can I tell the difference between producing more information and shaping better attention?

Second, can I name the judgment hidden inside a course, dashboard, or playbook before asking an agent to imitate it?

Third, can I see where portability transfers power, and where the people affected by the agent need appeal, refusal, or a human route?

The coach at the beginning has not disappeared. She has become more important, because someone has to decide what may travel, what must be tested, and what should remain in the room.

The useful question is no longer only “What can AI replace?” It is “Which forms of human craft become more important when intelligence can be called as a tool?”
