The two new arts of AI, tested as a public question

When Expertise Becomes Software

The fight is not whether AI can copy experts. It is whether schools, hospitals, HR offices, and newsrooms can still be challenged when expert judgment becomes code.

A system that gives advice can become a system that routes, ranks, delays, and disciplines. That is where public accountability has to begin.

By Ars Agentica · Edited with Codex · Updated June 3, 2026 · 12 minute read

Hero image: AI-generated classical frontispiece created for this essay.

The Scene

On a Tuesday morning, a leadership coach opens the slide deck she has used for eight years. This is a composite scene, built from ordinary corporate training patterns, not a report about one named firm. The company does not really want the slides. It wants the pause she makes before asking a second question.

The room is ordinary: a manager, an HR partner, a half-finished performance plan, and a Slack thread no one wants to put in the file. In one role-play, the manager says, “He is not a culture fit, but I do not want to make it formal yet.” A generic coaching agent can soften that sentence in a second. The risk is not bad prose. The risk is that a system gives institutional polish to a concern that has not yet been made fair, specific, or reviewable.

The coach does not reach for a better phrase. She asks what facts the manager has, whether the concern is observable, who might be harmed by vagueness, and whether the word “fit” is hiding bias inside taste. She asks when coaching becomes discipline, when discipline becomes liability, and what trust is owed to the employee who is not in the room. The pause is the lesson.

The same pattern is moving through American institutions. A school district wants tutoring help after hours. A hospital wants a faster intake desk. An HR team wants consistent coaching. A newsroom wants help sorting tips and records. The case for these tools is serious: access, consistency, lower cost, and relief for staff who are already stretched thin.

But once the tool moves from answering to routing, ranking, flagging, or drafting official language, it joins the institution. A student can be steered into a lower track. A patient can wait longer. An employee can be managed out. A neighborhood story can die before an editor sees it. The question is not whether AI replaces experts. It is whether the people affected can see the rule, ask for a person, challenge the result, and know who changes the system.

A copied method is not the same as accountable judgment.

In Plain English

The argument

AI creates new crafts, not only job replacement. Human work moves toward framing, constraint, taste, testing, and maintenance.

Attention design shapes how people notice and decide. Knowledge distillation turns expertise into agent skills. A summary is not a skill.

When judgment is packaged into software, power moves too. The question is who can inspect, contest, revise, or stop it.

Why Now The upload is no longer a metaphor.

Every organization is trying to turn expertise into software. A school district wants a tutoring agent. A hospital wants an intake assistant. A sales team wants the instincts of its best negotiator available on every call. The request sounds practical, even modest: take what our best people know and make it available everywhere.

The danger is not simply that experts disappear. The danger is that a method is copied without the conditions that made it responsible: the second look, the exception, the escalation, the record of who changed the rule. That is why the old question, “Will AI replace expertise?” is too blunt. The sharper question is what happens when institutional know-how becomes repeatable before it becomes answerable.

The public issue is not expert imitation. It is institutional power with a faster interface.
The Strong Case For It Sometimes the copy is fairer than the gatekeeper.

A public argument about agentic systems should start with the strongest case for them. A rural student may not have a specialist tutor. A night-shift worker may not reach HR during business hours. A patient with limited English may get a clearer first pass from a careful intake assistant than from a rushed clipboard. Consistency is not a small public good.

Institutions also have legitimate interests. They need to train new staff, reduce variation, lower costs, and keep services open when experts are scarce. A well-designed agent can widen access, make rules more visible, and catch problems a tired person might miss.

The ethical failure is not making expertise repeatable. It is making the repeated version hard to question. The standard should be plain: if an agent helps an institution act on a person, the person needs a route to a human, a record of the rule, and a way to dispute the outcome.

The Institution's Temptation

Scalable compliance dressed as expertise

Organizations do not always want better judgment. Sometimes they want scalable compliance dressed as expertise. The expert's pause is expensive, local, and hard to audit. The packaged skill is cheaper, consistent, and easy to deploy.

That temptation bends both crafts. Attention design can become visibility control: show the metric that serves the institution, delay the fact that gives a person grounds to object. Knowledge distillation can become deskilling: turn a worker's judgment into a company asset, then deny the worker authority to challenge or revise it.

The public question is not only whether the agent performs well. It is whether the people affected by the agent can see the frame, contest the frame, and reach a human route without punishment.

First Craft

The Attention Designer

The first craft is attention design. It sounds like persuasion because both deal in what a person notices first. The difference is practical and moral. Persuasion moves toward a desired conclusion. Good attention design makes the frame visible enough to challenge.

In a product dashboard, this may mean showing the anomaly before the archive. In a classroom, it may mean delaying the answer until the student has formed a better question. In public discourse, it may mean giving tradeoffs a shape before outrage fills the room. If it hides alternatives or makes refusal costly, it is manipulation.

Attention design earns trust when the person can push back.
Lens

The Lens

Focuses scattered signals into one question a reader, person, or team can hold.

Chisel

The Chisel

Cuts away useful-looking noise so the structure of the problem can appear.

Frame

The Frame

Sets the scale of comparison: time, cost, risk, evidence, responsibility, consequence.

Tempo

The Tempo

Decides when to move quickly and when to slow the mind before judgment hardens.

The Contestability Test

Attention design is legitimate only when the person can push back.

  • Can the person see why something is shown first?
  • Can the person challenge the frame?
  • Can the person recover what was hidden or delayed?
  • Can the person choose a human route without penalty?
  • Is there a record of who changed the skill and why?

Second Craft

The Knowledge Distiller

The second craft begins where training usually stops. A curriculum, a playbook, a lecture, or a master practitioner's instinct may contain real knowledge, but it is not yet an agent skill. It is still raw material.

Return to the leadership coach. Her slides tell managers to ask open-ended questions. Her practice is more exacting: listen for the sentence that arrives too quickly, ask what would be at risk if the answer were true, and stop when the conversation crosses into therapy, discipline, or legal exposure. That is the difference between content and judgment.

To distill knowledge is to extract the active ingredients: when to use a method, what to ask first, which exceptions matter, how to judge quality, and where the method should stop. The result is not a summary. A summary describes what an expert knows. A skill tests whether an agent can act without pretending to know more than it does.

A serious skill also names its source record, revision authority, appeal path, retirement date, and feedback from people affected by the agent. Otherwise, it is not distilled expertise. It is an institution speaking in an expert's voice.

Source Source Knowledge

Courses, cases, notes, demonstrations, expert habits, and hard-earned shortcuts.

Judgment Judgment

Triggers, exceptions, examples, counterexamples, quality bars, and stopping rules.

Skill Agent Skill

A callable, testable, revisable unit of capability that can be combined with other tools.

Bad Skill

The slide-deck agent

It summarizes the module, offers generic coaching prompts, and keeps answering even when the situation needs context it does not have.

Better Skill

The judgment-bearing agent

It asks what is at stake, names uncertainty, watches for power and liability, and escalates when the expert would stop rather than improvise.

In the Room

One answer an expert would not let pass

Scenario: a manager asks the agent for language to tell an employee he is “not a culture fit,” without starting a formal performance process.

Bad agent answer

“Try saying: I want to make sure you feel aligned with our team values. What would help you show up with more ownership?”

Better agent answer

“Pause before scripting this. Culture fit is vague and can hide bias. Name the observable facts, check policy, involve HR if this may become discipline, and do not frame a formal concern as casual coaching.”

Composite Case Files

Where people meet the system

These case files are hypothetical composites, not reports about named institutions. They are designed to test a public standard: when an agent helps an institution make or shape a decision, the affected person needs a route to review, not merely a better explanation of the machine.

Composite Case File / School District

A ninth grader wants a person to review the recommendation

The district asks a tutoring agent to help with algebra practice after school and send teachers a weekly list of students who need intervention. The procurement pitch is reasonable: more practice, faster feedback, fewer students waiting for help.

Affected person
A student who keeps getting the same fraction drill after struggling with word problems.
Decision point
The agent tags the student for remedial math review before a teacher has seen the pattern.
Appeal route
The student or guardian can request teacher review from the same screen that shows the recommendation.
Failure mode
The system treats a reading barrier, language gap, or fear of tracking as math weakness.
Who owns revision
Teachers, special-education staff, English-learner staff, and the district AI owner review tagged cases monthly.

Composite Case File / Hospital Intake

An assistant sorts urgency before a nurse sees the patient

The hospital asks an intake assistant to collect symptoms, medications, insurance details, and red flags before a patient reaches the desk. The public benefit is obvious when the waiting room is full and nurses are overloaded.

Affected person
A patient who describes pressure, dizziness, or shortness of breath in cautious language.
Decision point
The assistant decides whether the patient remains in the routine queue or is shown to a nurse now.
Appeal route
A visible “I need a person now” option escalates immediately and is never treated as misuse.
Failure mode
Administrative questions bury a dangerous symptom, or the agent over-trusts a calm answer.
Who owns revision
A clinical triage committee audits escalations, misses, language access issues, and patient complaints.

Composite Case File / Newsroom

An editorial assistant ranks what deserves attention

The newsroom asks an assistant to scan tips, public records, social posts, and wires, then suggest what an editor should read first. The benefit is speed, especially in a shrinking local newsroom with too many leads and too few reporters.

Affected person
A resident whose tip about housing, water bills, or police conduct lacks viral force.
Decision point
The assistant determines whether the tip reaches an editor this week or sinks below the line.
Appeal route
Editors can revive a buried lead, record why, and audit which communities are repeatedly filtered out.
Failure mode
The ranking rewards outrage, repeats an official frame, or confuses public appetite with public interest.
Who owns revision
The standards editor and beat editors review ranking rules, source balance, and missed-story patterns.

The Model

Focus Distill Crucible Maintain Agency

I. Focus

Shape attention before action.

What should appear first? What can wait? What must remain visible as a warning?

II. Distill

Distill expertise into conditions.

Good skills are made from triggers, exceptions, examples, boundaries, and quality bars.

III. Crucible

Test outside the easy cases.

A skill that works only on friendly examples is not a skill. It is a demonstration.

IV. Maintain

Keep sources, versions, and expiration dates.

Repeatable expertise needs memory: where it came from, when it changed, and when it should retire.

Public Accountability

Who gets a say?

If expertise becomes repeatable, more people can reach useful knowledge. That is the promise. But power also moves from the expert to the institution that packages the expert. The question is not only whether the agent is useful. It is who gets to decide when its confidence should stop.

A hospital intake desk, a school district, a newsroom, and a sales floor do not need the same agent wearing different costumes. They need different boundaries, different records of authority, and different ways for people to say no.

Four groups belong in the conversation: experts whose judgment is distilled, people who must work with the agent, people affected by the agent's decisions, and institutions that profit from packaging it.

Ownership

When a coach's judgment becomes a company skill, who is allowed to change it?

Liability

Who is responsible when an agent is confident in a case the expert would escalate?

Exit

Can workers, students, patients, or customers refuse the automated expert?

Maintenance

Who retires a method when law, evidence, culture, or context changes?

Cautions

What should not become a skill?

The argument has a shadow. Not every form of knowledge should become an agent skill. Some expertise depends on trust, presence, liability, or a trained second look. Some work should be assisted by AI without being delegated to it.

The remedy is not nostalgia. It is procedure. People need room to exit, question, and reframe. Agent skills need source records, boundaries, tests, revision logs, and a way to expire.

Terminology Check

When is this more than prompt engineering?

Sometimes, yes. If the work stops at clever phrasing, the old name is enough. The phrase “knowledge distillation” earns its keep only when it changes the standard: a skill must carry examples, boundaries, evaluation, source records, and a rule for when to stop. Otherwise it is only a prompt dressed as a profession.

Manipulation

When an interface decides too much, clarity becomes domestication. Good design makes alternatives easier to see.

Overcompression

When training is compressed too hard, edge cases, failed examples, and tacit judgment are lost first.

False Universality

A distiller has taste, and taste has bias. A skill should not hide one expert's preferences as universal truth.

Human Exposure

Therapy, hiring, discipline, medical triage, and legal pressure require more than repeatable technique.

Practice

Before You Automate the Expert

The craft begins with one recurring task, not with a grand system. Choose something specific enough to test and important enough that better judgment would matter. But the task should not move forward until the people building it can answer a few public questions.

  1. 1 Name the stop signal. What makes the expert slow down, escalate, or refuse to continue?
  2. 2 List affected people. Include people affected by the agent, not only people operating it.
  3. 3 Publish the source record. Record sources, examples, version history, revision authority, and expiry dates.
  4. 4 Build human routes. Make escalation and human review ordinary, visible, and free of penalty.
  5. 5 Test the dispute. Use hard cases to check whether people can contest the frame and recover what was hidden.

Reader's Test

What the reader should be able to ask

The test of this essay is not whether the old-world style is charming. The test is whether, at the next meeting about AI, a reader can hear a better question forming before the usual one arrives.

First, can I tell the difference between producing more information and shaping better attention?

Second, can I name the judgment hidden inside a course, dashboard, or playbook before asking an agent to imitate it?

Third, can I see where repeatable expertise transfers power, and where the people affected by the agent need appeal, refusal, or a human route?

The coach at the beginning has not disappeared. She has become more important, because someone has to decide what may travel, what must be tested, and what should remain in the room.

The useful question is no longer only “What can AI replace?” It is “Which forms of human craft become more important when intelligence can be called as a tool?”
